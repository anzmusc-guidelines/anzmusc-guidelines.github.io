About This Living Guideline

This guideline presents the best available, current scientific evidence for pharmacological management of the most common forms of inflammatory arthritis: rheumatoid arthritis (RA), psoriatic arthritis (PsA), and axial spondyloarthritis (axSpA).

As a living guideline, recommendations are updated in near real-time as new evidence emerges. Topics and questions identified as having highest clinical relevance are prioritised, including DMARD choice, switching, combination therapy, down-titration, and perioperative use.

Living Evidence

Recommendations updated as new practice-changing evidence becomes available.

GRADE Methodology

Rigorous evidence assessment using internationally recognised standards.

Expert Panel

Developed by rheumatologists, researchers, and consumer representatives.

Shared Decision Making

Supporting informed discussions between clinicians and patients.

Initial DMARD Therapy

Methotrexate combination therapy for rheumatoid arthritis

Recommendation
Evidence to Decision
Practical Info
For Consumers
â¬¥ Conditional Recommendation

Consider using methotrexate in combination with other DMARDs as initial therapy in people with rheumatoid arthritis. For most people this implies triple therapy (methotrexate + sulfasalazine + hydroxychloroquine).

Key Points

  • While the combination of methotrexate and most b/tsDMARDs is also effective, this is currently not a feasible option for most people due to PBS restrictions.
  • The choice between combination and monotherapy should be a shared decision based on individual values, preferences, and clinical status.
  • Future recommendations will focus on patients who have an inadequate response to initial therapy.

Summary of Evidence

The evidence was assessed using GRADE methodology. Multiple randomised controlled trials have compared methotrexate monotherapy with combination DMARD therapy in DMARD-naÃ¯ve patients with rheumatoid arthritis.

Benefits

  • Combination therapy may improve disease activity scores compared to monotherapy
  • May reduce radiographic progression
  • Earlier achievement of treatment targets

Harms

  • Increased pill burden with triple therapy
  • Potential for additional side effects from multiple medications
  • Monitoring requirements may be increased

Implementation Considerations

  • Triple therapy typically involves: methotrexate 10-25mg weekly + sulfasalazine 1-2g daily + hydroxychloroquine 200-400mg daily
  • Medications can be introduced simultaneously or in a step-up approach
  • Baseline investigations should include FBC, LFTs, renal function, and hepatitis serology

Monitoring

  • FBC and LFTs every 2-4 weeks initially, then every 8-12 weeks once stable
  • Ophthalmological screening for hydroxychloroquine as per guidelines
  • Regular assessment of disease activity using validated measures

What This Means For You

If you've been diagnosed with rheumatoid arthritis and are starting treatment, your doctor may recommend taking methotrexate together with one or two other medications (called "combination therapy" or "triple therapy").

Why Combination Therapy?

Research shows that using multiple medications together from the start may help control your arthritis better than using just one medication alone. This can help reduce pain and swelling, and protect your joints from damage.

Questions You Might Ask Your Doctor

  • "What are the benefits of taking these medications together?"
  • "What side effects should I watch out for?"
  • "How long before I notice an improvement?"
  • "What happens if this combination doesn't work for me?"
